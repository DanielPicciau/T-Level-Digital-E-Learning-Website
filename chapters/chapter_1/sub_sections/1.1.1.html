1.1.1 Problem Solving Approaches

Using computational thinkingA thinking process involving breaking down problems, finding patterns, focusing on important details, and creating step-by-step solutions. helps us understand complex problems and figure out how to create solutions.
These solutions need to be clear enough for a computer, a person, or both to follow.

When solving digital problems, three main strategies are often used:

  • Top-down
  • Bottom-up
  • Modularisation

Three Problem Solving Strategies

Top-Down Approach

This strategy starts with the main problem and breaks it down step-by-step (decompositionBreaking a complex problem into smaller, easier-to-handle parts.) into smaller sub-problems or modulesSelf-contained parts of a program or system. until each part is simple enough to solve easily.

  • Breaks big problems into smaller pieces.
  • Uses a step-by-step refinement process.
  • Work flows from the general goal downwards.
  • Often shown as a tree or hierarchy diagram.
  • Common in procedural programmingProgramming style focusing on sequences of instructions (procedures/functions), like in the C language..

Bottom-Up Approach

Acting as the inverse of the top-down strategy, the bottom-up approach begins by identifying and developing the most fundamental, low-level modulesSelf-contained parts of a program or system, often representing basic functionalities. or components of the problem first. These individual modules are designed, built, and often tested independently. Once these basic parts are functional, they are progressively combined or integratedThe process of combining smaller, tested modules or components into larger subsystems or the final complete system. to form larger, more complex subsystems. This process of combining and building continues upwards until all the parts are assembled into the final, complete solution for the original problem.

  • Starts with the basic building blocks/modules.
  • Focuses on combining finished parts (integration).
  • Builds up complexity towards the final goal.
  • Often used with Object-Oriented ProgrammingProgramming style using 'objects' that bundle data and actions (e.g., Python, Java). (OOP).

Comparing Top-Down and Bottom-Up

Aspect Top-Down Bottom-Up
Core Idea Splitting (Breaking down large problems) Merging (Combining small solutions)
Information Handling Can sometimes repeat information Avoids repeating information
Typical Languages Often Procedural/Structured (e.g., C) Often Object-Oriented (e.g., Python, Java)
Primary Focus Planning, structuring, step-by-step building Testing, especially how parts fit together (integration)

Top-Down: Starts big, gets detailed. Good when you know the overall goal clearly. Breaks the problem down (decomposition).

Bottom-Up: Starts small, builds up. Useful when the basic parts are clear, but the final structure might change. Puts pieces together (integration).

Modularisation: Creates reusable, independent blocks. Works well with both, especially OOP. Makes systems easier to manage and update.
