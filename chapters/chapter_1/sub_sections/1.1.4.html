1.1.4 Using Abstraction

What is Abstraction?

AbstractionThe process of removing or filtering out characteristics that are not needed, in order to focus on essential characteristics. is another key pillar of computational thinking. It involves identifying the essential information needed to understand or solve a problem while ignoring the irrelevant details.

Think of it like creating a summary or a simplified model. By removing unnecessary complexity, we can focus on the core aspects of the problem, making it easier to design a solution.

Abstraction in Action: The Tube Map

Iconic Example: London Underground Map

The famous London Underground map is a perfect example of abstraction. It shows:

  • Stations and their names.
  • The different lines (represented by colours).
  • Connections between lines.
  • The general sequence of stations on each line.

However, it deliberately filters out unnecessary details like:

  • The exact geographical location of stations.
  • The precise distance between stations.
  • The twists and turns of the actual tunnels underground.

By abstracting away these details, the map becomes much clearer and easier to use for its primary purpose: planning a journey from one station to another.

Benefits of Abstraction

Using abstraction effectively helps us to:

  • Reduce Complexity: Makes complex systems easier to understand and manage.
  • Increase Efficiency: Allows focus on the important parts, speeding up problem-solving and development.
  • Create Models: Provides a general, simplified view (modelA simplified representation of a system or problem, resulting from abstraction.) of the problem.
  • Avoid Errors: Focusing on irrelevant details can lead to incorrect solutions. Abstraction helps prevent this.
  • Improve Communication: Simplified models are easier to discuss and share with others.

General vs. Specific Details

Abstraction involves moving from specific details to a more general understanding. Consider making a casserole:

Creating Layers of Abstraction

Abstraction often involves creating layers, where each higher layer hides the complexity of the layer below it. Think of using a computer: you interact with the graphical interface (top layer), which hides the operating system (middle layer), which in turn hides the complex hardware operations (bottom layer).

There are two main steps when applying abstraction to solve a problem:

Determine exactly what information is essential to solve the problem. Ask yourself:

  • What data do I absolutely need?
  • Why is this information crucial?
  • What format does this information need to be in (e.g., text, number, date)?

Example: To calculate the area of a rectangle, you *need* the length and the width. You don't need its colour or location.

Actively remove or ignore all information that is not essential (identified in Step 1). Ask yourself:

  • What details are irrelevant to this specific problem?
  • Why can I ignore this information?
  • Will including this detail distract from or complicate the solution?

Example: When calculating the rectangle's area, filter out the colour, material, position, etc., as they don't affect the area calculation.

Elements Within an Abstraction Layer

When defining a layer of abstraction, especially in software development, it's useful to consider these elements:

  • Inputs: What data or signals does this layer receive? (e.g., user clicks, data from another module, sensor readings). Consider validation needed.
  • Outputs: What results or signals does this layer produce? (e.g., display on screen, data sent to another module, file saved). Consider the required format.
  • VariablesValues that can change during the execution of a program or process, often affected by inputs or calculations.: Data within the layer that can change (e.g., user score, current total, item count).
  • ConstantsValues within a program or process that do not change during execution (e.g., VAT rate, maximum attempts, fixed delivery price).: Data within the layer that remains fixed (e.g., PI, tax rate, company name).
  • Key Processes: The main actions or calculations performed by this layer (e.g., calculate total, validate input, search database).
  • Repeated Processes: Actions or calculations that occur multiple times within the layer or system (often candidates for functions or loops).

Abstraction and Other Pillars

Abstraction works closely with the other computational thinking pillars:

  • After decompositionBreaking down a complex problem into smaller parts., abstraction helps simplify each part.
  • Abstraction helps reveal underlying patternsIdentifying similarities or repeated processes. by removing superficial differences.
  • The simplified model created through abstraction is what we then design algorithmsCreating step-by-step instructions to solve the abstracted problem. for.

Effectively abstracting is crucial for managing complexity and developing clear, focused solutions.
